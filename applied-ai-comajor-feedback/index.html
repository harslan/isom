Questions and Concerns [cite: 18]

1. [cite_start]Integration with the SAIL Framework [cite: 19]

[cite_start]

This is probably my biggest question. [cite: 20] [cite_start]The SAIL Collaborative has spent two years building an institutional framework for AI literacy that won the 2025 Eduventures Innovation Award and was featured in AACSB Insights. [cite: 20] [cite_start]We’ve been running faculty development workshops, designing AI-enabled assignments, launching Prompt AIChemy competitions, and developing the BEAM kiosk concept—all toward the goal of embedding AI fluency across the curriculum. [cite: 21] [cite_start]The proposal doesn’t reference any of this existing infrastructure. [cite: 22] [cite_start]I’m not raising this as a turf concern—I’m raising it because SAIL provides a ready-made faculty development pipeline, pedagogical framework, and institutional credibility that could dramatically strengthen this proposal. [cite: 22] [cite_start]The question is: how does the co-major build on SAIL rather than run parallel to it? [cite: 23]

2. [cite_start]Curriculum Balance: Applied vs. Ethics/Policy [cite: 24]

[cite_start]

Of the five required courses, two are focused on ethics/regulation (AI Regulatory Frameworks and Ethics, Policy & Society with AI). [cite: 25] [cite_start]That’s 40% of the core devoted to policy and ethics. [cite: 26] [cite_start]These topics absolutely matter, but for a program called “Applied” AI, I’d expect the center of gravity to be more hands-on. [cite: 27] [cite_start]Students should be building things—training models, deploying workflows, evaluating tools in context. [cite: 28] [cite_start]A single, well-designed course could cover both the ethical and regulatory dimensions. [cite: 29] [cite_start]The freed-up slot could be used for a hands-on “AI Tools & Workflows” course where students actually build with AI agents, RAG pipelines, prompt engineering, and automation—the exact skills that differentiate applied graduates. [cite: 30]

3. [cite_start]The “Foundations of AI” Course Needs Sharper Definition [cite: 31]

[cite_start]

The proposal says this requirement can be met by existing CS or ISOM courses, or by a “new interdisciplinary course for CAS students who will not meet the pre-requisites.” [cite: 32] [cite_start]This creates a two-tier entry point where students in the same co-major may have very different foundational preparation. [cite: 33] [cite_start]What are the learning outcomes that every student must achieve, regardless of which course they take? [cite: 34] [cite_start]We need a common floor. [cite: 35] [cite_start]I’d recommend defining explicit competencies (e.g., can explain how a language model works, can evaluate model outputs for bias, can design a basic AI workflow) and then certifying which courses meet that bar. [cite: 35]

4. [cite_start]Credit Load and Interaction with Business Core [cite: 36]

[cite_start]

The co-major weighs in at 22–26 credits. [cite: 37] [cite_start]For SBS students who are already navigating a 56-credit business core (which we’re currently working to reduce to 47), this is a significant addition. [cite: 37] [cite_start]How does this interact with the existing major requirements and the 120-credit degree? [cite: 38] [cite_start]Can double-counting be applied more broadly—for instance, ISOM 201 already satisfies the business core and could count toward the co-major? [cite: 39] [cite_start]The proposal mentions the internship can double-count, which is good, but we should map out the full picture for students in different majors to ensure this is genuinely feasible within four years without overloading schedules. [cite: 40]

5. [cite_start]Faculty Capacity and Development [cite: 41]

[cite_start]

Who teaches these courses? [cite: 42] [cite_start]The proposal assumes faculty will volunteer to AI-tag their courses but doesn’t address the training and support needed to do this well. [cite: 42] [cite_start]This is exactly what the SAIL Friday workshops are designed for, and we’ve learned that faculty need structured support—not just encouragement—to meaningfully integrate AI. [cite: 43] [cite_start]The proposal should include a faculty development plan, possibly leveraging the existing SAIL workshop infrastructure, and should identify which departments will teach the core required courses. [cite: 44]

6. [cite_start]Financial Model Assumptions [cite: 45]

[cite_start]

The revenue projections assume 3 net-new students in Year 2 and 5 in Year 3, generating $281K and $543K net respectively. [cite: 46] [cite_start]I’d like to see the underlying assumptions: what tuition rate, what retention assumptions, what’s the cost basis? [cite: 47] [cite_start]More importantly, are we counting only net-new enrollments attracted by the co-major, or are we also accounting for existing students who adopt it? [cite: 48] [cite_start]The distinction matters for whether this is truly incremental revenue or reallocation. [cite: 49]

7. [cite_start]Governance: The PILOT Committee [cite: 50]

[cite_start]

The proposal references a “small PILOT committee” that reviews and approves AI-tagged course applications. [cite: 51] [cite_start]Who sits on this committee? [cite: 51] [cite_start]What are the criteria for approval? [cite: 52] [cite_start]How often does it meet? [cite: 52] [cite_start]This is a critical governance question because the quality and coherence of the co-major will depend entirely on how rigorously courses are vetted. [cite: 53] [cite_start]Without clear standards, we risk “AI-washing”—courses with superficial AI content getting tagged for enrollment reasons. [cite: 54]

8. [cite_start]Missing: Program-Level Learning Outcomes and Assessment [cite: 55]

[cite_start]

The proposal doesn’t include formal Student Learning Outcomes (SLOs) or an assessment plan. [cite: 56] [cite_start]For accreditation purposes—especially with AACSB and NECHE—this will need to be addressed. [cite: 57] [cite_start]What should graduates of this co-major be able to do that they couldn’t do with just their primary major? [cite: 58] [cite_start]Defining this clearly will also help guide course design and the AI-tagging criteria. [cite: 59]
